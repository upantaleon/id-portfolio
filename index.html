Positioning statement

My instructional design practice is grounded in three principles. Design begins with the learner, not the content: outcomes, assessments, and activities are sequenced to build the specific capacities learners need. Equity is structural: it shapes what gets assessed, how it gets assessed, and whose knowledge is represented. Collaboration with subject matter experts requires a design relationship, not a content handoff.

This practice spans Queen's University, OISE, Charles Sturt University, First Nations Technical Institute, and Six Nations Polytechnic across Ontario, Australia, and the Philippines.

The design problem

UDL in Ontario postsecondary contexts defaults to a technicist accessibility compliance model. Institutions implement CAST guidelines primarily to satisfy AODA obligations, with minimal interrogation of whose epistemologies are served by LMS architecture, rubric conventions, and content representation defaults.

The course required a design that teaches UDL critically while modeling it structurally, and that prepares learners to make analogous design decisions in their own practice. Three subsidiary problems were articulated before solution design began:

  • Standard UDL training evacuates race, Indigeneity, and colonialism from the accessibility conversation.
  • Ed-tech selection is typically driven by licensing agreements, not pedagogical or equity criteria.
  • Graduate assessment routinely privileges academic prose, creating credibility deficits for learners with oral, relational, or multimodal knowledge traditions.

Assessment design position

AI-detection software is not a defensible assessment tool. The research record establishes that detection tools produce false positives correlated with non-native English writing, and the outputs they flag are not reliably distinguishable from human writing by trained readers.

Recalibrated assessment answers a different question: can this student demonstrate the specific intellectual competencies required at the upper tiers of Bloom's Taxonomy, in relation to their specific professional context, in a way that requires their own positioned judgment?

Data-driven course improvement: Charles Sturt University case

At Charles Sturt, post-placement data from three sources (supervisor reports, student reflections, in-class observation notes) showed classroom management gaps were not a content problem but an assessment design problem. The assessment tested declarative knowledge, not applied judgment.

The redesign moved from written analysis to a case-based scenario with a decision-making component. The following cohort received substantially stronger supervisor ratings in targeted competencies. The revised assessment design was adopted by a colleague teaching a parallel subject.

This case is the primary answer to Q7: describe a time you used data to improve a course. Three-source data triangulation; gap diagnosed as assessment design failure, not curriculum or delivery; measurable cohort improvement in the targeted competency.

Project management evidence: FNTI VP Academic (Q5)

EDAII design evidence Q4 — equity, diversity, accessibility, Indigenization
Structural equity in design decisions, OCAP, UDL Disclosure, OEE framework
Design position on EDAII
EDAII cannot be an add-on layer applied after the course is designed. It has to be structural: in the learning outcomes, the assessment formats, the representation of knowledge sources, and the assumptions built into the course materials.

Concretely: assessments are designed with multiple modalities. Reading lists are audited for epistemic representation. Content at FNTI and Six Nations was built with and through community partners as co-authorship, not consultation. Rubrics make evaluation criteria explicit and transparent, reducing bias and giving marginalized learners clarity rather than ambiguity.

OEE framework applied to LMS analytics (Module 4 equity case)

The Onto-Ethico-Epistemological (OEE) framework, developed from Indigenous methodological traditions using the Medicine Wheel architecture, asks three questions of any knowledge system:

DimensionQuestionApplied to Canvas analytics
OntologicalWhat does it recognize as real?Course Analytics renders the student as a data subject: a collection of trackable behaviours. This is an ontological reduction. The student is a relational being whose engagement is distributed across family, community, land, and institution.
EthicalWhat obligations does it carry?Transparency (does the student know this data exists?), proportionality (is the action warranted by the data?), relational accountability (does the intervention strengthen or damage the instructor-student relationship?).
EpistemologicalWhat does it count as knowledge?Analytics embeds a behaviourist theory of learning. Incompatible with constructivist, relational, land-based, or oral-tradition pedagogical frameworks. A structural mismatch that must be named explicitly.

OCAP principles in technology evaluation (EDUC 6420, Week 6)

The critical technology evaluation framework in Week 6 requires graduates to assess any educational technology against OCAP principles (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) for Indigenous data sovereignty. This criterion is operationally absent from standard instructional design curricula and from most LMS vendor evaluations in Ontario. Its inclusion positions graduates to make defensible technology recommendations in Indigenous education contexts including midwifery programs, community colleges, and band-operated schools.

Q6
How would you approach training a faculty member who is resistant to adopting new learning technologies?
Start with the problem they are actually trying to solve, not the technology. Offer minimal viable version. Demonstrate rather than instruct. Connect with a peer who has had a positive experience. Be honest: if the technology does not genuinely improve student experience, say so. Trust is more valuable than compliance.
